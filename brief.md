# Happy List LA — Brand Brief

## The Prelude

Dating is expensive and people want to meet in person. Happy hour solves both — a $32 date beats a $150 one when you're still figuring out if you like someone.

The reframe isn't affordability. It's formality. Happy hour signals casual intent: no reservation, no outfit decision, no three-hour commitment. You're not budgeting. You're choosing to be informal.

Happy hour used to be a stop on the way home from the office — a commute, a clock-out, a 5pm trigger. None of that exists. People work their own hours, free at 2pm or not starting till evening. The day is modular now, and happy hour fits any open slot the way no other social ritual does.

Which makes it something different: the way out of the apartment. For a city full of people working alone from home, happy hour is the lowest-friction path back into public life — a booth, a good drink, another person across the table.

Restaurants hemorrhage money from 3 to 6. Dating apps need users to meet in person. Remote workers need third places. Happy hour sits at the intersection of all three, and no one has built the infrastructure to connect them.

## SOAP (Strategy On A Page)

```
BUSINESS GOAL:     Become LA's happy hour platform — the place consumers check
                   weekly and restaurants list with to fill off-peak seats.

BUSINESS PROBLEM:  No one owns happy hour as a category. The data exists (menus,
                   prices, hours) but it's scattered across outdated listicles
                   and Yelp pages. No curation, no distribution, no habit loop.

CONSUMER GOAL:     Get out of the apartment, sit across from someone, try a
                   place — without the friction of planning a full dinner.

CONSUMER PROBLEM:  Happy hour still carries a "cheap fallback" stigma, and the
                   old trigger (leaving the office at 5pm) is dead. People
                   don't think of happy hour as something you choose — they
                   think of it as something that used to happen to you.

INSIGHT:           Happy hour isn't a budget move — it's the lowest-friction
                   social act in a city full of people working alone from home.
                   The value isn't the $7 cocktail. The value is the booth.

SMP:               Make it happen.
```

## Creative Brief (GET / WHO / TO / BY)

**GET:** LA residents 25-40 with flexible schedules — remote, hybrid, freelance, gig — who eat out regularly but default to the same spots and treat going out as a nighttime activity requiring planning.

**WHO:** Want to meet people, try places, and feel like participants in their city, but the gap between "I should go somewhere" and actually leaving the apartment keeps widening — especially for low-stakes outings that don't justify the coordination effort of a full dinner.

**TO:** Check Happy List weekly and treat happy hour as an intentional, repeatable part of their social life — not a fallback but a first choice.

**BY:** Showing them that happy hour is modular, informal, and available right now — a curated $32-40 date at a place they'd never commit to for dinner, with the exact order already built for them. **Make it happen.**
